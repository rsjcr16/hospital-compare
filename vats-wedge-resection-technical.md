## Surgical Indications

### Oncologic Indications

**Primary Lung Cancer**
- Stage IA NSCLC ≤2 cm (T1a-T1b)
- Peripheral lesions with adequate surgical margin achievable
- Patients with limited pulmonary reserve (FEV1 <60% predicted)
- Ground glass opacities or minimally invasive adenocarcinoma
- Patients declining or medically unfit for lobectomy

**Metastatic Disease**
- Solitary or limited pulmonary metastases
- Controlled primary tumor
- Adequate disease-free interval
- No extrathoracic disease
- Colorectal, sarcoma, renal cell metastases (most common)

**Diagnostic Indications**
- Indeterminate pulmonary nodule requiring tissue diagnosis
- Failed bronchoscopic biopsy
- Peripheral lesions not amenable to percutaneous biopsy
- Need for architectural assessment (not possible with core biopsy)

### Non-Oncologic Indications
- Bullectomy for spontaneous pneumothorax
- Lung volume reduction surgery (LVRS)
- Benign nodules (hamartoma, infectious granulomas)
- Interstitial lung disease biopsy

## Pre-operative Assessment

### Pulmonary Function Testing

**Essential Studies**
- Spirometry with FEV1 and FVC
- DLCO (diffusion capacity)
- Arterial blood gas
- Six-minute walk test if borderline function

**Risk Stratification**
- **Low Risk**: FEV1 >80%, DLCO >80%
- **Moderate Risk**: FEV1 60-80%, DLCO 60-80%
- **High Risk**: FEV1 <60%, DLCO <60%
  - Consider quantitative V/Q scan for predicted post-op function
  - Calculate ppoFEV1 and ppoDLCO

**Formula for Predicted Post-Operative Function:**
```
ppoFEV1 = preop FEV1 × (1 - % functional lung tissue removed/100)
```

### Cardiac Evaluation
- EKG for all patients
- Echocardiogram if history of cardiac disease
- Stress test for >2 cardiac risk factors
- Cardiology consult for significant disease

### Imaging Requirements

**CT Chest with IV Contrast**
- Thin-cut protocol (1-2mm slices)
- 3D reconstruction for surgical planning
- Assessment of:
  - Tumor size, location, relationship to fissures
  - Pleural involvement
  - Vascular anatomy
  - Lymph node status
  - Ground glass vs. solid component ratio

**PET-CT**
- Standard for all confirmed or suspected malignancies
- SUV max for risk stratification
- Identify occult metastases
- Lymph node assessment (SUV >2.5 concerning)

**Brain MRI**
- Stage IB or higher NSCLC
- Any neurologic symptoms
- Consider for high-risk histologies (adenocarcinoma, large cell)

### Mediastinal Staging

**EBUS/EUS Indications**
- Clinical N1, N2, or N3 disease on imaging
- Central tumors
- Tumors >3 cm
- PET-positive lymph nodes

**Mediastinoscopy**
- Enlarged lymph nodes not accessible by EBUS
- Negative EBUS with high clinical suspicion
- N2 disease requiring confirmation before neoadjuvant therapy

## Surgical Technique

### Patient Positioning
- Lateral decubitus position
- Affected side up
- Axillary roll to protect brachial plexus
- Flex table to open intercostal spaces
- Arms positioned at 90° on arm board
- Secure with bean bag or tape

### Anesthesia Considerations
- Double-lumen endotracheal tube or bronchial blocker
- Single-lung ventilation
- Arterial line for monitoring
- Foley catheter
- Consider epidural for post-op pain management

### Port Placement

**Standard Three-Port Technique**
1. **Camera Port** (typically 10mm)
   - 7th-8th intercostal space, mid-axillary line
   - Initial entry point
   - 30° angled thoracoscope preferred

2. **Anterior Working Port** (typically 10-12mm)
   - 4th-5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line
   - Primary instrument port
   - Stapler insertion port

3. **Posterior Port** (typically 5mm)
   - 8th-9th intercostal space, posterior to scapula tip
   - Retraction and additional instruments
   - Can be utility incision if needed

**Utility Incision Variant**
- 4-6 cm incision, 4th-5th intercostal space
- Anterior axillary line
- Use wound protector
- Direct finger palpation of lung
- Reduced trauma vs. thoracotomy

### Intraoperative Steps

**1. Initial Exploration**
- Systematic inspection of entire pleural cavity
- Identify tumor location
- Assess for pleural disease
- Check for adhesions
- Bimanual palpation (if utility incision)

**2. Nodule Localization**
- Direct visualization (most peripheral lesions)
- Digital palpation (utility approach)
- Intraoperative ultrasound (deep lesions)
- Preoperative localization techniques:
  - CT-guided wire placement
  - CT-guided dye injection (methylene blue, indocyanine green)
  - Radiotracer injection with gamma probe
  - Electromagnetic navigation bronchoscopy

**3. Wedge Resection**

**Stapling Technique:**
- Elevate target area with graspers
- Position endoscopic stapler perpendicular to parenchyma
- Ensure adequate margin (minimum 1 cm or tumor diameter, whichever greater)
- Sequential stapler firings as needed
- Typical cartridge: 60mm length, green or gold reloads

**Technical Considerations:**
- Avoid excessive tension on tissue
- Ensure complete staple line formation
- Reinforce staple line if air leak present
- Consider biological sealants for air leak prevention
- Place specimen in retrieval bag

**4. Margin Assessment**
- Send fresh specimen to pathology
- Request frozen section analysis
- Measure closest margin grossly
- Ink specimen margins
- Re-resect if margin inadequate (<1 cm)

**5. Hemostasis and Air Leak Check**
- Irrigate pleural cavity
- Lung re-expansion test
- Water seal test for air leaks
- Argon beam coagulation for minor bleeding
- Additional sutures or clips as needed

**6. Chest Tube Placement**
- 24-28 Fr chest tube
- Position in apex for air, base for fluid
- Secure with purse-string suture
- Connect to water seal/suction (-20 cm H2O)

**7. Closure**
- Remove ports under visualization
- Close muscle layers
- Subcuticular skin closure
- Local anesthetic infiltration

### Robotic-Assisted Technique

**Setup**
- Patient positioning similar to VATS
- Robot docked from patient's back
- Three-arm configuration typical

**Port Placement**
- Camera port: 8th intercostal space
- Working arms: 5th and 9th intercostal spaces
- Assistant port: 4th intercostal space

**Advantages**
- Enhanced 3D visualization
- Improved instrument articulation
- Tremor filtration
- Ergonomic for surgeon

**Disadvantages**
- Loss of tactile feedback
- Longer operative time (learning curve)
- Increased cost
- Docking/undocking time

## Intraoperative Decision-Making

### Conversion to Thoracotomy

**Absolute Indications**
- Inability to safely collapse lung
- Massive bleeding (>500 mL)
- Major vascular injury
- Inability to identify/localize tumor
- Inadequate exposure for safe resection

**Relative Indications**
- Dense adhesions limiting visualization
- Unexpected locally advanced disease
- Tumor proximity to major vessels
- Need for complex reconstruction
- Frozen section showing more aggressive histology requiring lobectomy

**Conversion Rate Benchmarks**
- Experienced centers: 3-5%
- Community practice: 5-10%
- Higher rates acceptable during learning curve

### Extent of Resection Decisions

**Proceed with Wedge if:**
- Adequate margins achievable (≥1 cm)
- Peripheral location
- Frozen section confirms NSCLC (not small cell)
- Patient factors favor limited resection

**Consider Segmentectomy if:**
- Central lesion requiring excessive parenchymal resection for wedge
- Anatomic segmentectomy provides better oncologic margin
- Equivalent pulmonary function preservation
- Lesion in anatomically favorable segment

**Convert to Lobectomy if:**
- Inadequate margins with wedge
- Frozen section shows aggressive histology
- N1 disease discovered intraoperatively
- Tumor involves intersegmental plane
- Vascular involvement requiring more extensive resection
- Small cell lung cancer (not suitable for wedge)

## Complications and Management

### Intraoperative Complications

**Hemorrhage**
- **Minor bleeding** (<200 mL): Observation, cautery, hemostatic agents
- **Moderate bleeding** (200-500 mL): Additional sutures, clips, identify source
- **Major bleeding** (>500 mL): Convert to thoracotomy, vascular control

**Common Sources:**
- Pulmonary artery branch injury
- Intercostal vessel injury during port placement
- Adhesions with chest wall vessels

**Air Leak**
- **Minor leak**: Observation, biological sealants
- **Moderate leak**: Additional sutures over staple line
- **Major leak**: Re-staple, pleural tent, consider prolonged chest tube

### Post-operative Complications

**Prolonged Air Leak (>5 days)**
- **Incidence**: 3-5%
- **Risk Factors**: COPD, emphysema, steroid use, apical resections
- **Management:**
  - Water seal trial at day 3-5
  - Heimlich valve for ambulatory management
  - Consider Pneumostat if persistent >7 days
  - Chemical pleurodesis if >14 days
  - Surgical intervention (VATS pleurodesis) if >21 days

**Post-operative Atrial Fibrillation**
- **Incidence**: 5-7%
- **Prevention**: Beta-blockers in high-risk patients
- **Management:**
  - Rate control (beta-blocker or calcium channel blocker)
  - Cardioversion if hemodynamically unstable
  - Anticoagulation if sustained >48 hours
  - Consider amiodarone if refractory

**Pneumonia**
- **Incidence**: 3-5%
- **Risk Factors**: Age >70, COPD, prolonged surgery, aspiration
- **Prevention:** Incentive spirometry, early mobilization, pulmonary hygiene
- **Management:** Cultures, appropriate antibiotics, aggressive pulmonary toilet

**Empyema**
- **Incidence**: <1%
- **Risk Factors**: Prolonged chest tube, contamination, immunosuppression
- **Management:**
  - Early stage: Antibiotics, chest tube drainage
  - Organized: VATS decortication
  - Chronic: Open thoracostomy if needed

**Recurrent Pneumothorax**
- **Incidence**: 1-2%
- **Management:**
  - Small (<20%): Observation, oxygen
  - Large or symptomatic: Chest tube reinsertion
  - Persistent: Re-exploration

## Oncologic Considerations

### Margin Adequacy

**Current Standards**
- **Minimum acceptable margin**: 1 cm OR diameter of tumor (whichever greater)
- **Optimal margin**: >2 cm when possible
- **Margin ratio**: Margin distance/tumor diameter >1

**Impact of Margin Status:**
- Positive margins: Significantly increased local recurrence (30-50%)
- Close margins (<1 cm): Increased recurrence risk (15-25%)
- Adequate margins (≥1 cm): Acceptable local control (5-10% recurrence)

**Frozen Section Protocol**
- Request frozen section on all specimens
- Assess closest margin
- Measure distance from tumor to staple line
- Re-resect immediately if inadequate

### Lymph Node Sampling

**Controversial in Wedge Resection**
- Not routinely performed in all centers
- Arguments FOR sampling:
  - Accurate staging
  - Identify N1 disease requiring adjuvant therapy
  - Prognostic information
  
- Arguments AGAINST routine sampling:
  - Increased operative time
  - Higher complication rate
  - Wedge typically for Stage IA disease (low N+ rate)
  - May not change management in limited resection candidates

**Recommendation:**
- Sample suspicious nodes intraoperatively
- N2 stations if PET-positive preoperatively
- Hilar (N1) nodes if accessible and suspicious
- Consider sampling in younger, fit patients

### Oncologic Outcomes

**Wedge vs. Lobectomy Data**

**Classic Studies:**
- **Ginsberg RJ (LCSG 821)**: Higher local recurrence with wedge (lobectomy 5% vs. wedge 18%)
- **CALGB 140503**: Non-inferiority trial (segmentectomy vs. lobectomy for tumors ≤2 cm)

**Current Evidence:**
- Tumors <1 cm: Equivalent outcomes
- Tumors 1-2 cm: Conflicting data, likely acceptable in selected patients
- Tumors >2 cm: Lobectomy superior
- Pure ground glass: Wedge may be adequate
- Part-solid: Consider segmentectomy or lobectomy

**Risk-Benefit Analysis:**
- Operative mortality lower with wedge (0.5% vs. 1.5% for lobectomy)
- Local recurrence higher with wedge (10-15% vs. 5% for lobectomy)
- Pulmonary function better preserved with wedge
- Consider patient age, comorbidities, life expectancy

### Adjuvant Therapy Considerations

**Indications Post-Wedge Resection:**
- Positive margins: Re-resection preferred; radiation if not possible
- Close margins (<1 cm): Consider radiation therapy
- N1 disease: Adjuvant chemotherapy
- High-risk features: Lymphovascular invasion, poor differentiation
- Stage IB (tumor 3-4 cm): Consider adjuvant chemotherapy

**Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy (SBRT)**
- Alternative to surgery in inoperable patients
- Salvage for positive margins
- Emerging data on SBRT + surgery

## Evidence Base and Outcomes

### Key Clinical Trials

**CALGB 140503 (Ongoing)**
- Comparing segmentectomy vs. lobectomy for Stage IA NSCLC ≤2 cm
- Non-inferiority design
- Results pending but preliminary data suggests equivalence

**JCOG0802**
- Japanese trial: Segmentectomy vs. lobectomy for tumors ≤2 cm
- Published 2022: 5-year OS 94.3% (segmentectomy) vs. 91.1% (lobectomy)
- Demonstrated non-inferiority of segmentectomy

**JCOG0201**
- Examined extent of lymphadenectomy
- Systematic nodal dissection vs. sampling
- No difference in survival

### Meta-Analyses

**Wedge Resection Outcomes (Pooled Data)**
- 5-year overall survival: 65-75% (Stage I disease)
- 5-year disease-free survival: 55-65%
- Local recurrence: 10-20%
- 30-day mortality: 0.5-1%
- Major complications: 8-12%

**VATS vs. Open Approach**
- Equivalent oncologic outcomes
- VATS advantages:
  - Reduced post-op pain
  - Shorter hospital stay (2-3 days vs. 5-7 days)
  - Faster return to normal activity
  - Lower complication rates
  - Better pulmonary function preservation
  - Improved quality of life

### Quality Metrics

**Society of Thoracic Surgeons (STS) Benchmarks**
- 30-day mortality: <1.5%
- Major complication rate: <8%
- Conversion to thoracotomy: <5%
- Readmission rate: <5%
- Average length of stay: ≤3 days

## Technical Innovations

### Localization Technologies

**Electromagnetic Navigation Bronchoscopy (ENB)**
- Preoperative or intraoperative marking
- Fiducial placement near lesion
- Dye or microcoil injection
- Success rate: 85-95% for peripheral lesions

**Indocyanine Green (ICG) Fluorescence**
- Near-infrared imaging
- Injected preoperatively (CT-guided or bronchoscopic)
- Intraoperative fluorescence visualization
- Excellent contrast for small/deep nodules

**Radiotracer Localization**
- Technetium-99m injection (CT or bronchoscopic)
- Intraoperative gamma probe detection
- Particularly useful for ground glass opacities

**Intraoperative Ultrasound**
- Real-time localization
- No preoperative preparation
- Limited by depth and lesion characteristics
- Operator-dependent

### Surgical Innovations

**Uniportal VATS**
- Single 3-4 cm incision
- All instruments through one port
- Potential advantages:
  - Reduced intercostal nerve injury
  - Less post-operative pain
  - Improved cosmesis
- Learning curve considerations

**Robotic-Assisted VATS**
- Da Vinci robotic platform
- Enhanced visualization (3D, 10x magnification)
- Articulating instruments
- Growing adoption for wedge resections

**Biological Sealants**
- Fibrin glues
- Synthetic sealants (PEG-based)
- Collagen patches
- Evidence for air leak reduction mixed
- Cost considerations

**Energy Devices**
- Ultrasonic scalpel (Harmonic)
- Bipolar vessel sealing (LigaSure)
- Argon beam coagulation
- Microwave ablation (for margin enhancement)

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Protocols

**Key Components:**
1. Pre-operative patient education
2. Minimal fasting (clear liquids until 2 hours pre-op)
3. No routine bowel preparation
4. Multimodal analgesia (reduce opioids):
   - Paravertebral blocks or epidural
   - NSAIDs
   - Acetaminophen
   - Gabapentin
5. Minimally invasive technique
6. Early chest tube removal
7. Early mobilization (within 4 hours)
8. Early oral intake
9. Structured discharge criteria

**Outcomes with ERAS:**
- 1-2 day reduction in length of stay
- 50-70% reduction in opioid use
- Decreased complications
- Improved patient satisfaction
- Cost savings

## Summary for Practicing Physicians

**Key Points:**
1. VATS wedge resection is appropriate for selected patients with Stage IA NSCLC, particularly tumors <2 cm
2. Adequate surgical margins (≥1 cm) are critical for oncologic outcomes
3. Pre-operative localization techniques improve success for small/deep lesions
4. VATS approach offers equivalent oncologic outcomes with improved perioperative outcomes vs. thoracotomy
5. ERAS protocols significantly improve recovery metrics
6. Patient selection is paramount - balance oncologic adequacy with functional preservation
7. Conversion to more extensive resection should not be hesitated when oncologic principles dictate
8. Ongoing trials will further define optimal extent of resection for early-stage disease
